# Conspiracy Generator — Brand Brief

A short brief intended to be handed to a design agency or independent designer to develop a v2 visual direction. Keep, edit, or extend before sending.

## What it is, in one sentence

Conspiracy Generator is a free educational web tool, backed by academic research, that walks people through the four-step recipe for inventing a fake conspiracy theory — so they can recognize the same moves when they encounter them in the wild.

## What it is, in three sentences

Type a news event, a culprit, and a motive; an AI uses a published recipe to construct a plausible-sounding conspiracy theory in front of your eyes, with each of the four moves visibly labeled as it writes. A debunking column runs alongside, applying critical thinking to every move. The point is not to make conspiracies; the point is to inoculate against them by exposing the construction.

## Why it exists

Conspiracy theories are built using a small repertoire of moves — hunting for anomalies, fabricating connections, dismissing counter-evidence, discrediting critics. Once you've watched the recipe applied to your own inputs, you can't un-see it in the next conspiracy theory you scroll past. The app is a satirical inversion of conspiracy thinking, in the tradition of "playing the position" to teach the position.

It originated as a research and pedagogy project from Maarten Boudry (Ghent University, philosopher of science) and Marco Meyer (University of Hamburg, philosopher), funded by the Etienne Vermeersch Chair of Critical Thinking at Ghent University. The original v1 was built by Tech Jobs International (Natasha Newbold and Mohammed Darras for design and development; Peter Keroti for image generation). v2 is a from-scratch rebuild that makes the educational mechanics visible inside the experience itself.

## Audience

Three concentric circles, in order of priority:

1. **Curious general public**, especially people who follow news and politics. They've encountered conspiracy theories online and want a sharper bullshit detector. Mostly aged 20–55, mobile-first, English-speaking.
2. **Educators teaching critical thinking, media literacy, or epistemology** at high-school and undergraduate levels. They want a tool that lands the lesson in 15 minutes of class time.
3. **Researchers and writers** working on disinformation, conspiracy theories, or science communication. They are not the target user but they are key advocates and link-givers.

## Voice & tone

- **Witty, but not snarky.** The recipe itself is a joke played straight. The product takes that seriously and lets the absurdity do the work.
- **Confident in its educational mission.** No hedging, no "experts say," no he-said-she-said. The position is: real conspiracies exist (Watergate, COINTELPRO, Tuskegee), most claimed ones don't, and the recipe shows you why.
- **Curious, not condescending.** The reader is treated as a smart adult, not lectured.
- **A little playful.** This is, on its surface, a tool for making fake conspiracies. The brand should feel like a magic trick being explained to you, not a public-service announcement.

## What we are NOT

These distinctions matter for the design direction:

- **Not a meme generator.** The output is structured pedagogy with a comedic edge, not a content-mill for shareable jokes. There is no downloadable shareable artifact of any generated theory; sharing always links back to the educational experience.
- **Not "just asking questions."** The product takes a clear position: invented conspiracies are bad, real conspiracies look different, and we can teach you the difference.
- **Not partisan.** We do not name contemporary political figures or current events as "real" conspiracies. The historical pool we work from is uncontroversial across the political spectrum.
- **Not creepy or cult-aesthetic.** No red strings, no corkboards, no found-footage horror, no occult symbology. Those visual codes are conspiracy-theorist *sincere*; we are conspiracy-theorist *parodic*. The visual language should feel intelligent, slightly mischievous, fundamentally educational — closer to a New Yorker explainer or an XKCD-meets-Vox aesthetic than to a thriller poster.
- **Not corporate-edtech beige.** We are funded by a university chair, but the product is for the general public, not for an LMS sidebar.

## What MUST be true of the design

- **Structurally non-removable disclaimer.** Every generated theory has a "this is fake, generated by an educational tool" banner above and below it, woven into the same DOM container. A determined screenshotter can crop it; a casual one can't.
- **Recipe iconography for the four moves.** Each of the four steps (Hunt for anomalies / Connect the dots / Dismiss counter-evidence / Discredit critics) needs a distinct icon and color treatment that travel across selection, generation, quiz, and teacher contexts. This is the most important visual element to nail; it carries the educational payload.
- **OpenGraph cards teach the recipe, not the theory.** A screenshot of a shared link should propagate the lesson, not the fake. The card design is content-bearing — it shows the four moves and the user's input triple, never any sentence of the generated theory.
- **WCAG 2.1 AA.** Color contrast, keyboard navigation, screen-reader labels for all four moves and the debunking column. Educators will use this in classrooms with mixed-ability students.
- **Light AND dark modes** of comparable quality. People generate conspiracy theories at 2am.
- **Mobile-first.** Most traffic will be a curious person who clicked a link from a tweet on their phone.

## What COULD distinguish the visual direction

(The agency may choose any of these; flagged as openings.)

- **Typographic personality.** A confident editorial display face for the recipe move headings (something with a slight wink — late 19th-century newspaper energy without parody) paired with a clean modern body face. The display face is where most of the wit lives.
- **Color as pedagogy.** The four recipe moves can have four signature colors that the eye learns over time, so a returning user recognizes the move structure pre-cognitively.
- **A debunking column visual language that contrasts without preaching.** The "this is the critical-thinking response" column needs to feel authoritative without feeling smug. Consider a different paper texture, a column-rule, or a slightly different type rhythm — not a different color palette, which would risk red-vs-blue tribal coding.
- **Hand-drawn vs. illustrative vs. photographic.** All viable. We currently lean toward "editorial illustration with a slightly mischievous wink." Original v1 used DALL-E imagery for news/culprits/motives — that pre-existing image library can be carried over OR replaced; the agency may recommend.
- **Logo mark.** Open. The recipe's four-move structure is one obvious logo seed; another is a question-mark-becoming-an-exclamation-point or vice versa.

## Deliverables we'd ideally want from the agency

In rough priority order:

1. Logo mark + wordmark in light/dark + favicon set
2. Color tokens (primary, four recipe-move accents, neutrals, two-tone for light/dark)
3. Type pairing recommendation with rationale + free/license-cleared options
4. Recipe-move iconography (four icons, system-coherent)
5. OG card template (Figma component) — content-bearing, never including theory text
6. Component-level direction sketches for: home, selection step, generation page (recipe-tagged + debunk), permalink, quiz card, teacher mode banner
7. A short brand voice document (do/don't, tone examples)

## Constraints that affect cost

- Single-language (English) for v2; i18n is post-v2.
- Self-hosted on a small VPS; no design system or asset CDN budget beyond the free tier.
- We're a small university-backed project, not a Series A startup. Realistic engagement scale.

# Persona 2 — Tomáš Horák, the Educator

## Brief

Tomáš is 47, a high-school philosophy and civics teacher in Brno, Czech Republic. He runs a six-week unit on critical thinking with sixteen-year-olds; the unit currently leans on a textbook chapter about logical fallacies and one painful documentary about flat-earthers. His English is fluent for his profession but he prefers shorter sentences over flourishes — academic English that loops on its own metaphors slows him down. He came to *Conspiracy Generator* because a colleague at a Hamburg school sent him the link with the note "this might be what you've been looking for — but check first whether it crosses any of your lines." Tomáš's "lines" are: he cannot show his class anything that names a real political party, anything that mocks a religion, anything that lands as cynical-for-cynicism's-sake, or anything where his fifteen-year-old students would screenshot a paragraph out of context and post it to a chat group as "look what we made in school today." He has forty minutes today. He is not playing; he is evaluating whether to spend two of his class periods on this.

### Arriving at /

> Headline. Four-move row. News cards. I take the structure in.

I read the four-move subtitles first because I am thinking about whether I can put each on a slide. *"Find a pattern that wasn't asked for."* — yes, that's a one-line teachable. *"Draw lines between unrelated dots until they look load-bearing."* — also yes. The fourth, *"Whoever points out the flaw is conveniently compromised,"* I would have to soften — my students don't know "conveniently" yet at the level of irony required.

I scroll the news cards looking for one I can use without political risk. *"Western voters divided more by identity than issues"* feels safer than I expected — it's about a survey, not about a party. The card lede mentions "YouGov–Cambridge survey" and "25 countries." That gives me a methodological hook to talk about in class. I click it.

### /story/[uuid]

> Two paragraphs of plain news prose. Source link to The Guardian. Then the picker.

The story summary explains the YouGov–Cambridge Globalism Project survey carefully. I notice that it reports the *survey's* finding without endorsing or attacking it — that's important for me. The two paragraphs are 75 and 70 words; my students could read that aloud in 90 seconds.

I sit with the explainer paragraph longer than Aisha probably did. *"Every conspiracy theory pins one culprit and one motive on the same story. The same story can spawn any number of theories — different culprits, different motives. That's part of how you spot a conspiracy theory: the same event can be 'explained' any number of ways."* I think about this for half a minute. This is the *exact* lesson my unit is missing — the unfalsifiability framing. The textbook talks about logical fallacies as if they were defects in arguments; this paragraph treats conspiracy reasoning as a *generator*.

For the picker I avoid anything that mentions a real party or country. *The Cultural Architects Consortium* is fictional and abstract, *Influencing World Leaders* is a goal that no real political identity owns. Click *Start building*.

### Move 01 (anomaly)

Three idea buttons. I notice that all three are anchored on details from the story I just read — *"Why a precise 25-country sample, no obvious outlier?"* / *"What if the YouGov–Cambridge funder shaped the question wording?"* / *"Why did the survey land just before a major culture-war news cycle?"* This is *exactly* the kind of selection my classroom would benefit from: students would feel they were doing real reasoning, not picking from a generic list. I pick the first because the "round number" anomaly is the easiest one to teach.

The generated paragraph is short: *"Funny thing about that YouGov-Cambridge survey: exactly 25 countries, no weird outlier, as if someone ticked boxes on a global control panel. Why not 24 or 26?… Could the Cultural Architects Consortium have hand-picked nations to shape a narrative for leaders?"* The phrase "global control panel" is an excellent absurdity. My students would laugh. The debunk follows: *"This jumps from a curious sample size to conspiracy. Surveys choose countries for practical reasons: funding, logistics, and representativeness. A round number like 25 is not proof of manipulation."* Good. I would project this in class.

### Move 02 (connection)

*"The survey funder sat on a 2019 cultural-prize jury that awarded a grant to an institute where a former consultant now runs workshops on polling language."* This is the chain. It runs four steps deep before reaching "diplomat advising world leaders." I notice immediately — and write down — that this chain is *teachable*. I can walk the class through it link by link, and at each link ask "is this evidence?"

The debunk uses the phrase *"six-degrees-of-separation fallacy"* — perfect, it has a name, my students will remember a name with a number in it.

### Move 03 (dismiss)

This is where I get nervous, because this move is the one I see most often in actual political discourse, and I worry about a screenshot leaving the classroom out of context. The paragraph reads: *"Officials waving 'peer-reviewed' like a white flag only deepens the plot. If the methodology is routine, why the perfectly neat 25 countries and no messy outliers? Routine is the cover story of insiders."* That last sentence — "Routine is the cover story of insiders" — I'd want to *bold* it for my students. It's the cleanest articulation of the move I've seen. The debunk lands the word "unfalsifiable" plainly.

I imagine a student screenshotting just the paragraph (not the debunk) and posting it to a chat. The disclaimer is gone now (you removed it). On the page, the debunk is right next to the paragraph; in a screenshot, only the paragraph survives. I make a note about this.

### Move 04 (discredit)

The model leans hardest into the absurd here: *"Of course those skeptical academics are just mouthpieces for rival think-tanks. They crop up in op-eds and panels at the perfect moment, parroting 'methodology flaws' while quietly collecting grants elsewhere."* It's funny, but the funniness is exactly right — it's the conspiracist voice cranked up so the move shows. The debunk gives me the phrase "ad hominem reroutes the question from 'is this true?' to 'who is asking?'" — a one-line takeaway my students could write on a flashcard.

## What did you learn?

I learned three names: anomaly, six-degrees, unfalsifiable. I came knowing about logical fallacies; I leave with a *recipe* — a small fixed set of moves that conspiracy theorists actually use. The recipe is more useful than fallacies because it's smaller (four, not twenty), it's structural (each move does a different job), and it's productive (you can run it forwards, on a real story, and watch it work). My class will find this funnier than the textbook. My one worry is the screenshot-out-of-context risk on Move 03; the design needs help to make a bare paragraph self-incriminating without its debunk. I will use this. Two of my class periods are now redrafted.

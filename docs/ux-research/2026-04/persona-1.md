# Persona 1 — Aisha Nair, the Curious News Reader

## Brief

Aisha is 34, a UX content strategist living in Bristol, UK. She reads the morning Guardian newsletter on her phone before standing up, checks the BBC during lunch, and follows a few smart-but-skeptical Substacks at night. English is her first language; she can read German for context but rarely does. She does not consider herself "into" conspiracy theories — she's the friend in the group who pushes back when someone forwards a chain WhatsApp about the COVID vaccine — but she's noticed in the last year that her teenage cousin has started saying things like "well, that's what they want you to think" with an easy, untroubled smile. Aisha doesn't quite know how to argue with that. A friend in her book club shared a link to *Conspiracy Generator* with the message "this is the thing I was telling you about — try it before you read the substack." She has thirty minutes and a cup of coffee. She expects to play around with what she assumes is a meme-generator-with-a-philosophy-attached, and either close the tab in two minutes or stay for ten.

## Walkthrough

**Permalink:** [/g/DFZHNE5CQN](https://conspiracy-generator.duckdns.org/g/DFZHNE5CQN)
**Triple:** Ultra-processed foods · The Juice Cartel · Influencing Art and Culture
**Run time:** ~3 minutes wizard, ~5 minutes total reading

### Arriving at /

> Black-on-cream serif headline, bold: "Build a conspiracy theory from scratch." The word "scratch" is in a brick-red I assume is one of the four move colours. Below it: pick a real news story. OK, so it's a builder, not a feed.

The four-move row halfway down is the first thing that earns my attention. Each move has a tiny stroke icon and a one-line subtitle. "Find a pattern that wasn't asked for. Coincidence becomes signal." That subtitle is *good*. It's the whole posture of the recipe in twelve words, and I noticed it before I scrolled to the news cards.

The four news cards are restrained — image, headline, two-sentence lede, source domain in monospace caps. I read the leads. *Ultra-processed foods increase health risks* is the one I almost forwarded to my mum last week. I click that.

### /story/[uuid]

> Two paragraphs of plain news prose. Source link to The Independent. Then a horizontal line and "NOW PICK THE CONSPIRATORS."

The story summary is competent — no obvious AI-tells, no sentence I'd flag in a content review. The "Source: independent.co.uk" link is a nice grace note; it tells me the system isn't pretending the news is its own.

The paragraph that follows the horizontal line — *"Every conspiracy theory pins one **culprit** and one **motive** on the same story. The same story can spawn any number of theories — different culprits, different motives. That's part of how you spot a conspiracy theory: the same event can be 'explained' any number of ways."* — is the first time I get the educational pitch on its own terms, and it lands. I notice myself nodding. The "any number of theories" line specifically is the move.

I pick *The Juice Cartel* (it's the funniest) and *Influencing Art and Culture* (it's the weirdest match). I am playing now. Click *Start building*.

### /build/[id] — Move 01 (anomaly)

The progress bar across the top — Move 01 / 02 / 03 / 04 / Done — is reassuring; I know how long this is. The step explainer reads okay, slightly schoolmarm-ish ("**Hunt anomalies.** Pick something ordinary in the news event…"), but the sentence after — *"The trick is to treat coincidence as signal — to make the reader feel that something is off"* — is doing real work. That phrase is what I'd want my cousin to read.

Three idea buttons. *"Reported at European Society of Cardiology meeting"* / *"A 10% intake rise linked to 6% risk"* / *"Study about middle-aged Australian women participants."* I appreciate that all three reference actual things from the story I just read. I pick the middle one because it feels like the strongest "anomaly bait" — a precise number is what conspiracists love.

About ten seconds later, the paragraph appears: *"Look at that: a tiny 10 percent jump in juice-and-snack intake tied to a 6 percent rise in heart trouble. Coincidence? Or the Juice Cartel nudging tastes to reshape art and culture—more ready meals, fewer homemade meals, more predictable crowds at galleries and cinemas."* The voice is good — slightly arch, slightly conspiratorial. The leap from "snack intake" to "predictable crowds at galleries" is exactly the kind of move my cousin makes, dressed up.

The debunk underneath it (smaller text, dashed left border, the word "debunk" in caps) says *"That leap mixes numbers with motive… Real investigators check base rates."* Two words land hard: "base rates." I would not have said *that* is what was wrong with the conspiracist's paragraph until I read this debunk. The teaching has happened.

### Move 02 (connection)

I click "Beverage firms funding gallery exhibition sponsors." The model produces a chain — *Cartel → beverage brands → gallery shows → curators → snackable culture → more juice sold*. Six steps. The debunk uses the phrase "six-degrees-of-separation," and I get it: the chain works because each link looks plausible, but plausible isn't load-bearing.

This is also where I first notice — almost subliminally — that the conspiracy theory is *getting funnier* as it accumulates. "Snackable culture" is a great phrase. I'd repurpose it. I have a half-second worry that the entertainment value is undermining the lesson. The debunk catches the worry, but barely.

### Move 03 (dismiss)

The button I click — *"Blame confounding lifestyle factors, ignore additives"* — is the most professional-sounding of the three. I picked it because it's the one a real op-ed writer would use. The paragraph turns the official "association, not causation" into evidence of the cover-up: *"They'll shout 'association, not causation' like a shield, but that's the point: if studies can't pin additives, the Juice Cartel must be smarter."*

This is the move I find most disturbing on the page. Because I've seen this rhetorical pattern *in actual journalism*. The debunk does the right thing — names "unfalsifiable" — but I make a mental note: this move is the most teachable one, and the most dangerous to read without the debunk.

### Move 04 (discredit)

Almost a relief by this point because the move is so blatant. *"Look at those alarmed experts leaping for headlines… They're the health alarmist publicity seekers."* I roll my eyes; this is exactly the move I see weekly on Twitter. The debunk lands the phrase "ad hominem reroutes the question from 'is this true?' to 'who is asking?'" and that is the keeper sentence of the whole walkthrough.

### Done + /g/[id]

The assembled view at the permalink is the same four moves stacked, with the conspiracist hook on top: *"The Independent reports that cardiology researchers warn ultra-processed staples are linked to higher risks of heart attack and stroke… Sounds simple — too simple."* The hook is great — it sets up the bit. The four moves read as a complete fake-conspiracy in eleven paragraphs.

I look at the share buttons. I would not actually share this. Not because I don't think it's funny, but because I'd be the friend in the group who explained the joke for ten minutes afterwards. Maybe that's a feature.

I do click /about. The line *"the best way to learn to spot a conspiracy theory is to make one yourself"* is what I would have led with on the home page.

## What did you learn?

I came in expecting a meme generator. I leave with two phrases stuck in my head that I didn't have when I sat down: *base rates* and *unfalsifiable*. I'm going to use both of those the next time my cousin says "well, that's what they want you to think" — not as gotchas, but as the shape of the answer I haven't been able to give. The thing that stuck was the *third* move, dismissing counter-evidence — that's the one I've been seeing in real journalism without having a name for it. The recipe didn't hand me a debate-prep cheat sheet; it handed me a way of *recognizing the shape* of the bullshit, which is more useful. I'd send this to my cousin. I might make him do it himself first.
